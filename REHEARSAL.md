# Kipchimatt Online — Rehearsal Script

> The pitch with delivery cues. Stage directions in **[brackets]**. Pause marks (`▼` long, `·` short). Tone tags `{calm}`, `{warm}`, `{firm}`, `{quiet}`.
>
> **Practice this out loud at least 4 times before the meeting.** Twice alone, twice in front of a partner. Time yourself with a stopwatch.
> Target: **7 minutes from open to "what questions do you have."**

---

## BEFORE YOU WALK IN

**Night before**
- Charge laptop to 100%. Pack charger.
- Test the Pages URL on the actual laptop you'll use. Test on hotel/office wifi.
- Open `DECK.html` and `index.html` in two browser tabs. Pin them.
- Print 5 copies of `LEAVE-BEHIND.html` (A4, single page).
- Print one copy of this script. Highlight your weak sections.

**Morning of**
- Eat. Don't pitch hungry.
- Wear what makes you feel grounded. Not new clothes — clothes you've worn confidently before.
- Arrive 20 min early. Test the projector. Check the HDMI/cable.
- Drink water. Have a glass at the table within reach.

**Five minutes before they walk in**
- Stand up. Roll shoulders back twice. Take three slow breaths.
- Say out loud: *"I am not selling this. I am sharing what I built. They are smart people. We are on the same team."*
- Smile at the empty room. Reset your face.

## DURING Q&A

**Posture**
- Body open, hands visible. Don't cross arms.
- When asked something, look at the questioner first, then broaden eye contact to the room as you answer.
- Drink water between questions if you need a beat to think. The water glass is your friend.

**Tone**
- Lower your voice when challenged. Resist the urge to match aggression.
- "That's a sharp question" is fine — once. Don't say it twice in the same meeting.
- "I don't know yet, but I'll find out and come back to you in 48 hours" is a *strong* answer. It is never weak.

**The four phrases that buy you time**
1. *"Let me make sure I understood that — you're asking…"* (rephrase the question, you'll hear yourself thinking)
2. *"Two parts to that answer."* (forces structure)
3. *"My honest answer is…"* (signals candor)
4. *"What I'd want to test in the pilot is exactly that."* (turns objection into pilot value)

**If you get a question you genuinely cannot answer**
> "That's a sharp question. I don't want to guess in front of the board. Let me come back to you with a written answer within forty-eight hours."

Then immediately move on. Don't apologize twice. Don't be defensive.

**If a director says no**
- Don't argue. *"Help me understand what would change your mind."*
- Listen. Take notes. Repeat back what you heard.
- Close with: *"Thank you. I'll come back with a revised proposal that addresses those concerns."*

**If a director says yes**
- Don't celebrate. Just nod and say: *"Asante. Let's confirm next steps."*
- Then go straight to the checklist on slide 7. Get the three sponsors named *in the room*. Don't leave with "we'll figure that out later."

## WHAT TO DO WITH YOUR HANDS

- Default position: lightly clasped at the navel, or one hand resting on the table.
- When making a point, open the palms slightly. Don't point fingers at people.
- Counting off — index finger, middle, ring — works once or twice. More than three counts looks rehearsed.
- Avoid: pen-clicking, hair-touching, ring-twisting, hands in pockets.

## WHAT TO DO WITH YOUR EYES

- 2-3 second hold per face. Then move.
- Do not stare at the senior director. They feel it. Spread the attention evenly.
- When you reach a punch line — silence + steady eye contact with one face — earns ten times more than the words.

## WHAT TO DO WITH YOUR VOICE

- Drop, don't raise, when you want emphasis. Quiet is power.
- Slow down on numbers. *Six. To. Nine. Months.* — these are the numbers they'll remember.
- Smile through the warm sections (story, demo, close). Neutral through the firm sections (problem, ask).
