## 1. THE 7-MINUTE PITCH

> Below is the script. Don't read it word-for-word — internalise the beats. Time yourself against the marks.

### 0:00 — OPEN WITH A STORY (30 seconds)

> "Last Saturday, my sister-in-law in Eldoret was buying her monthly shopping. She had ten items in mind, but she ended up making three trips that day — two to the supermarket, one to the *duka* — because between her, my brother, and their mother who came for the weekend, nobody had a single shared list.
>
> By the third trip, the shopping was costing them more in *boda* fares than the items were worth. And every shilling they spent that day went to a competitor. Not us.
>
> I'm here to talk about how we stop losing those shillings."

*(Pause. Look around the table.)*

### 0:30 — THE PROBLEM, FRAMED FOR US (60 seconds)

> "Three things are happening in Kenyan retail right now, and we are caught in the middle of all of them.
>
> **First**, our customers are increasingly shopping by phone. Not just for electronics — for unga, for sukari, for nyama. Naivas, Carrefour and Quickmart all have apps. Glovo and Bolt-Food are pushing supermarket delivery hard. We are the only major supermarket in the North Rift without an online presence.
>
> **Second**, when our own customers do shop online from us, they're doing it through Glovo or Bolt — and Glovo is taking 18 to 25 percent of every order. That's our margin disappearing into Spain.
>
> **Third**, we have something they don't: 25 years of trust, 11 stores across the North Rift and Nandi, our own bakery making Kipchimatt Bread, our own Mbogo Tea, our own Valley Sugar, our own water. We have *brand*. We're just not putting it to work online."

### 1:30 — THE DEMO (3 minutes — this is the engine)

*(Open the laptop. Show the URL on the projector or pass the laptop around.)*

> "What you're looking at is a working prototype of Kipchimatt Online. Not a slideshow — a real, clickable app. I built this in [time taken] to show you exactly what we'd ship.
>
> Let me show you six things that no other supermarket in Kenya does."

**Click through these in order, ~30 seconds each:**

1. **Spin-the-wheel** — *"First-time visitors get a discount. Costs us nothing. Drives daily app opens."*
2. **Pamoja Cart** — *"This is the headline feature. One shared cart for a whole family or chama. Mum adds maziwa from her phone. Baba adds nyama from his. The avatars on the cart show who added what. No competitor has this. It's our moat."*
3. **Mpishi (recipe-to-cart)** — *"Pick pilau ya nyama. Slide servings up to 8. Tap Add. Five ingredients land in the cart, sized perfectly. We just sold an entire shop on a single tap."*
4. **Bei Yangu** — *"For the customer who has KSh 2,000 this week. The slider builds her a smart basket — pantry essentials first, then fresh, then dairy. Maximum nutrition per shilling. This is for the 70% of Kenyans who shop to a budget every week."*
5. **Tuma Nyumbani** — *"For the diaspora and for working Nairobians who have folks upcountry. Send a shop to mum in Kapsabet. Add a voice note. We deliver from our nearest branch. Every Kenyan I've shown this to says 'finally.'"*
6. **Lipa Pole Pole** — *"Three M-Pesa instalments on anything over KSh 5,000. The Samsung 43-inch TV that nobody has the cash for becomes the Samsung TV they take home today. Big-ticket sales jump."*

**Then close the demo with two more:**

7. **Bei Tracker** — *"On every product, a 30-day price chart. We're saying to the customer: we are not hiding inflation from you. We will earn your trust by showing you when prices have come down."*
8. **Mama Mboga Pickup** — *"Our customer collects from a kiosk on her street. Mama earns KSh 30. We save the boda fee. And the political narrative that big retailers are crushing mama mbogas? We just inverted it."*

### 4:30 — WHY US, WHY NOW (60 seconds)

> "Here is why this works for us specifically, and not for Naivas or Carrefour:
>
> - We are **owner-operated**. We can decide today and ship in 16 weeks. Naivas needs three committee meetings just to pick a font.
> - We have **11 branches concentrated in one region** — Kericho, Bomet, Nandi, North Rift. Same-day delivery here is logistically simple. Trying to launch a Nairobi-first app means competing with five well-funded incumbents on day one.
> - We have **house brands**. Kipchimatt Bread, Mbogo Tea, Valley Sugar, Mbogo Water, Keben Tea. Online lets us put those at the top of search results in our own app. We can't do that on Glovo.
> - We are **debt-free** and we just celebrated 25 years. The board's appetite for the next chapter is real.
>
> Why now? Because every month we wait, our customers form habits with someone else. The cost of catching up rises with every quarter."

### 5:30 — THE BUSINESS CASE (60 seconds)

> "Conservative numbers. Our 11 branches see roughly 22,000 walk-ins a day combined — counting that the Mini and Express formats are smaller than the Mains, Primes and Heights.
>
> If even **1 percent** of those customers migrate to online in year one, that's 220 online orders a day. Industry data says online baskets run 30% larger than walk-in. Conservatively, KSh 2,500 per online order.
>
> That's KSh 550,000 a day in online revenue. KSh 16.5 million a month. **About KSh 175 million in year one**.
>
> At a 5-7% net margin after delivery and payment fees, that's roughly **KSh 9-12 million in year-one profit**.
>
> The investment we're asking for to start this is KSh 3.5 million. The pilot pays itself back in **4 to 6 months**. From month 7 onwards, every shilling is incremental margin to the group.
>
> And those numbers do not yet count: the loyalty data we'll capture for the first time, the diaspora revenue from Tuma Nyumbani, the BNPL volume from Lipa Pole Pole, or the mama mboga pickup fees."

### 6:30 — THE ASK (30 seconds)

> "I am asking the board for three things.
>
> **One**: KSh 3.5 million, ringfenced as a Phase 1 pilot budget — broken down line by line in the document I'll share.
> **Two**: Permission to launch in Eldoret Serani Mall and Litein, our two highest-traffic branches.
> **Three**: A working sponsor group of three directors who will meet me every two weeks for 30 minutes to unblock decisions.
>
> If we say yes today, the app is live for customers by [date 16 weeks out]. Within 12 months we'll have full data on whether to scale to all 11 branches.
>
> The downside is bounded. The upside is the next 25 years."

### 7:00 — CLOSE

*(Pause. Don't fill the silence.)*

> "What questions do you have?"

## 5. EIGHTEEN ANTICIPATED QUESTIONS

> Read these out loud. Tighten an answer until you can deliver it in under 60 seconds. The boardroom respects calm short answers more than long defensive ones.

### Q1. Naivas, Carrefour, Quickmart and Glovo all have apps. Why will ours work where theirs are middling?

> "Three reasons. **One**: we are the only one designing for Kenyan family shopping behaviour, not copying a Western app. Pamoja Cart and Mpishi do not exist anywhere else. **Two**: we own our region. Same-day delivery from 8 nearby branches is genuinely faster than Naivas trying to deliver from Westlands to Eldoret. **Three**: we are not asking customers to switch their whole shop online. The pilot says: do your weekly top-up online, the rest however you like. Lower friction, higher conversion."

### Q2. How much will this really cost us — including the parts you haven't told us about?

> "Three numbers. **KSh 3.5M** to build and launch the pilot — itemised in the budget. **KSh 250k–350k per month** to operate it from month 5 onwards: hosting, customer support, ongoing development, payment fees, marketing. **And there's a third number you should hear**: KSh 0 for the option to walk away after month 4 if the pilot misses every floor metric. We don't compound losses on a dud."

### Q3. What if it flops?

> "The Option B budget is ring-fenced and time-boxed. We have go/no-go gates at week 10 and week 16. If the alpha at week 9 shows the product fundamentally doesn't work, we stop at the contingency line — total exposure roughly KSh 2.2M, not 3.5M. The product photography and brand assets remain our IP. The store-level customer database remains ours. We learn, we close, we move on. Worst-case loss is recoverable in two months of bakery profit."

### Q4. Who is going to actually run this? You alone?

> "Phase 1 needs three people: me as lead, one backend engineer, one part-time designer. From month 5, we add one customer support agent and one operations lead — both can be drawn from existing branch staff who know our products. By month 12 the team is six people. **I am asking to be accountable for the outcome. I am not asking to do all the work alone.**"

### Q5. Why now? Why not in two years when the market is more mature?

> "Because the market matures with someone else's customers. Once your wife buys her unga from Quickmart for six months, that habit takes us a year of marketing to break. Habit formation is the asset competitors are accumulating right now. Today is cheaper than tomorrow — every quarter we wait, our acquisition cost rises."

### Q6. Will online cannibalise our in-store sales?

> "Some of it, yes — and that's fine, because the alternative is they cannibalise our sales by going to Naivas instead. Industry data: online customers tend to *increase* their total spend with the same retailer by 15–25% within 12 months because the convenience makes them shop more frequently. Online doesn't replace in-store — it's a second channel for the same customer. We measure this in the pilot."

### Q7. How does this work with our existing POS?

> "Phase 1: a daily CSV export from the POS uploads into the app's inventory database. Simple, boring, reliable. Phase 2: a real-time API bridge — we have it scoped for month 7. We do not need to replace any in-store system to launch."

### Q8. What about returns and refunds?

> "Same policy as in-store — fresh produce returnable within 24 hours, packaged 7 days, electronics 14 days — but with the convenience of *bring it to any of our 11 branches*. Refunds reverse via M-Pesa for STK-paid orders, in cash at the branch otherwise. We provision a 1.5% returns reserve in the budget."

### Q9. What about cyber security? What if we get hacked?

> "Three layers. **One**: we never store M-Pesa PINs or full card numbers — payments go through Safaricom's Daraja and a PCI-compliant card gateway. **Two**: we'll do a security audit before public launch — KSh 80k, included in the legal line. **Three**: we'll register with the Office of the Data Protection Commissioner in week 3. We carry standard cyber insurance from the second month of operation."

### Q10. Who owns the customer data?

> "Kipchimatt Enterprises Ltd. owns it. The data is hosted on infrastructure we control. No third-party marketing cookies. Customers can request export and deletion under the Data Protection Act. This is the *largest* asset we'll create from this project — bigger than the app itself. After 12 months we'll have a profile of every active customer's preferences, frequency and price sensitivity."

### Q11. What if Safaricom raises Daraja fees?

> "Daraja transaction fees are bands by volume — at our pilot scale we sit in the second band, around KSh 11–15 per transaction. If Safaricom raises by 50% (which they haven't done in five years), our payment cost per order rises by KSh 5–7. We'd absorb that in margin or pass it on as 'service fee' on orders below KSh 1,000. Manageable."

### Q12. Who delivers the orders? Are we hiring riders?

> "Two-track. **For the pilot**: 8 riders on contract through a partner like SafeBoda or a local Eldoret operator. We brand them in our colours but don't carry the HR. **For Phase 2 if we scale**: bring delivery in-house at the branches that justify the volume. Same model Naivas uses."

### Q13. Why don't we just deliver via Glovo / Bolt and skip the cost?

> "Two reasons. **One**: Glovo takes 18-25% of every order. On a KSh 175M annual run-rate, that's KSh 32-44M of margin we keep by going direct. The investment pays for itself just on this. **Two**: Glovo doesn't give us the customer data. They are a marketplace — they own the customer relationship. We become a no-name supplier. With our own app, every customer is *our* customer."

### Q14. How is this different from us just having a Facebook page and taking WhatsApp orders?

> "Order volume scaling. WhatsApp ordering caps out at maybe 30–50 orders a day before the team manning the phone breaks down — we know, we tried it during COVID. The app handles 1,000 orders a day with the same headcount. WhatsApp also doesn't carry payment, doesn't carry inventory accuracy, doesn't carry analytics. The app keeps WhatsApp as a *channel* — customers can still order via WhatsApp 0700 547 244 — but it's a frontend on the same backend."

### Q15. What happens if you (the developer) leave?

> "Two protections. **One**: all code is in our company GitHub from day one — not on my laptop. We use mainstream technologies (Node.js, PostgreSQL, Next.js) that any Kenyan dev can pick up. The codebase is documented. **Two**: the backend developer we hire from month one is hired with a written knowledge-transfer plan in their contract. By month 4 there are at least two people who can run the system."

### Q16. What does success look like in the first 90 days specifically?

> "Concretely, by day 90: **at least 800 daily active users at Eldoret Serani Mall, 30+ daily paid orders, 70% M-Pesa share of payments, average basket of KSh 1,800+, and a 25% repeat-order rate within 30 days.** We track these in a public dashboard the directors can see live. If by day 90 we are below floor on three of these five, we trigger a structured review."

### Q17. Boards spend money on tech and watch it disappear all the time. How is this not that?

> "Three answers. **One**: we are spending less than the cost of one bakery refit. KSh 3.5M is small money for a group with our portfolio. **Two**: it's gated. The contract pays out by milestone — week 6, week 10, week 16. If we miss a milestone, the next tranche doesn't release. **Three**: there is *something to see* every two weeks. Director sponsors meet with me, click the latest version on a real device, and judge for themselves whether progress is real. There is no black box."

### Q18. What do you need from us right now, today, in this room?

> "Four things. **One**: a yes-in-principle that the budget is real. **Two**: three director sponsors who'll meet me every two weeks for 30 minutes. **Three**: introductions to the Eldoret Serani Mall and Litein branch managers — they'll be the operational anchors. **Four**: a date for the next meeting where I bring the signed contracts and the kickoff plan. Two weeks from today is ideal."

## 6. RISK REGISTER

> Show the directors you've thought about what can go wrong.

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| M-Pesa Daraja onboarding takes longer than expected | Medium | Medium | Apply in week 1, parallel-track Card payments via Pesapal |
| Adoption is slower than the floor numbers | Medium | High | Free first delivery, 10% off first order, in-store sign-up kiosks at the two branches |
| Riders quit or under-perform | Medium | Medium | Contract with established rider operator (SafeBoda) for surge capacity |
| Cyber incident / data leak | Low | Critical | Security audit before launch; cyber insurance; no PCI data stored |
| Inventory drift between online and in-store | High | Medium | Daily inventory reconciliation cron; 'order pending stock check' state for low-stock items |
| House-brand stock-outs disappoint customers | Medium | Medium | Reserve 5% safety stock at branches that ship online orders |
| Family/political pushback against mama mboga model | Low | Low | Position as partnership not displacement; share PR plan early |
| Lead developer leaves | Low | High | Code in company repos, paired hire from month 1, documented |
